Evolution of HyperText

Origins:

HyperText was first conceived of by computer scientist Ted Nelson in the 1960s. Nelson envisioned a system of non-linear information storage and retrieval that would allow for the creation of a new type of literature. He called this system "Xanadu," and it was intended to be a way of organizing and linking information in a way that was not constrained by the linear structure of traditional books and documents. While Nelson's vision of Xanadu was never fully realized, his ideas were influential in the development of the World Wide Web, which is based on the principles of HyperText.

Evolution:

The concept of HyperText has evolved significantly since its inception. The World Wide Web, which was created by Tim Berners-Lee in the early 1990s, is based on the principles of HyperText and has revolutionized the way that information is organized and accessed. The Web has made it possible to publish and access information in a way that is not constrained by the limitations of traditional print media, and has enabled the easy dissemination of information to a global audience. The Web has also given rise to new forms of literature and art that are based on the principles of HyperText, such as hypertext fiction and interactive art installations.

Impact:

The impact of HyperText on the way that information is organized and accessed cannot be overstated. The Web has revolutionized the way that information is published and accessed, and has made it possible to create and share information in a way that was never before possible. HyperText has also had a significant impact on the way that literature and art are created and consumed, and has given rise to new forms of creative expression that are based on the principles of non-linear information storage and retrieval.
